2) Top Stories

June jobs report stumbles: +57,000 payrolls, hiring cools sharply

Nonfarm payrolls rose just 57,000 in June, well below the ~115,000 consensus, and April–May figures were revised down by a combined 74,000. The unemployment rate held at 4.2%, but labor-force participation fell 0.3 point to 61.5%, the lowest since March 2021, and leisure & hospitality shed 61,000 jobs.

Why it matters: The soft print all but takes a July Fed rate hike off the table — odds of at least one hike this year fell to about 76% from 84% — reshaping the outlook for borrowing costs into the fall.

Bonds rally as weak jobs data dims Fed rate-hike expectations

Treasurys rallied after the payroll miss, with short-term yields falling and the 10-year easing to about 4.47% after testing 4.5% earlier in the session. Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh separately said U.S. inflation risks were softening. BMO's head of U.S. rates strategy said the data makes it "difficult to envision a path toward a July Fed hike."

Why it matters: A Fed on hold rather than hiking changes the math for mortgages, corporate borrowing, and equity valuations heading into the July 28–29 FOMC meeting.

Trump ties housing bill to SAVE America Act, discloses $2B+ in 2025 income

In an Oval Office interview with CNBC, President Trump gave a lukewarm answer on signing the bipartisan housing bill that cleared Congress last month, saying "I'd rather not sign anything until we sign the SAVE America Act." He also released a financial disclosure showing he earned more than $2 billion in 2025, saying son Eric handles his finances.

Why it matters: A stalled housing bill leaves a major affordability package in limbo while the White House uses it as leverage for its voting-legislation priority.

U.S. opts not to renew USMCA, shifts to rolling trade talks

The administration decided against renewing the USMCA trade pact, moving instead to open-ended rolling negotiations with Mexico and Canada. The move preserves an implicit threat of roughly doubling tariffs on both neighbors; Trump had soured on the deal because it shielded large swaths of trade from tariffs and did little on trade deficits.

Why it matters: Persistent tariff uncertainty over America's two largest trading partners clouds costs for autos, agriculture, and manufacturing supply chains.

Why it matters: A rare bipartisan transparency win that could expose past settlements and reshape accountability rules on Capitol Hill.

Venezuela earthquake aftermath: U.S. aid deploys amid deportation controversy

Following the June 24 magnitude ~7.5 earthquakes that killed more than 2,290 people in Venezuela, the State Department is deploying a Disaster Assistance Response Team and urban search-and-rescue teams. Rep. Joaquin Castro condemned ICE for attempting to deport Venezuelan families from a Texas detention facility during the disaster; the families were returned after being flown to Arizona.

Why it matters: The disaster is testing U.S. sanctions and immigration policy toward Venezuela and could drive new migration pressure at the border.
