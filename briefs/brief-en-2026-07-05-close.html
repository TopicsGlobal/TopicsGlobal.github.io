DOGE officially sunsets after 18 months, far short of $2 trillion goal

The U.S. DOGE Service Temporary Organization terminated on July 4 under the sunset written into Trump's January 2025 executive order, without issuing a final report. DOGE claimed about $215 billion in savings from job cuts, canceled contracts and asset sales; Reuters had reported it effectively disbanded as a centralized entity months earlier. Trump called the sunset "the perfect gift" for America's 250th birthday.

Why it matters: The formal end closes a chapter that pushed thousands of federal workers out and voided billions in contracts — but agencies, courts and inspectors general will be sorting out its legal and budgetary aftermath for years.

June jobs report cools sharply, easing pressure on the Fed

Nonfarm payrolls rose just 57,000 in June — the smallest gain in four months and well below the roughly 110,000–115,000 consensus — while unemployment held at 4.2%. The miss broke a three-month hot streak and led traders to slash rate-hike bets: odds of a July move fell to about 20%, and September odds dropped to 55% from 64%.

Why it matters: A cooler labor market strengthens the case for the Fed to stay on hold, a key swing factor for mortgage rates, Treasury yields and this month's FOMC meeting.

Fragile U.S.–Iran ceasefire keeps oil near multi-month lows

The Pakistan-mediated ceasefire that paused the U.S.–Iran conflict has been violated repeatedly by both sides, but shipping through the Strait of Hormuz continues to recover and traders remain hopeful a permanent peace deal can be reached. Brent crude sits near its lowest levels since late February, unwinding the war-driven spike that had fed U.S. inflation worries.

Why it matters: Cheaper oil is a direct tailwind for U.S. consumers and a disinflationary force the Fed is watching — but any ceasefire breakdown could reverse it quickly.
